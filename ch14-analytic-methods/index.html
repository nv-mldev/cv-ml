The Question¶

“We’ve been simulating everything. When can we use formulas instead?”

All previous chapters built understanding through simulation — we shuffled, resampled, and generated. But many textbooks give you formulas directly.

When are those formulas valid? And when does simulation win?

Normal Distributions — The Special Case¶

Many analytic results only hold when data is normally distributed.

If X∼N(μ,σ2)X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)X∼N(μ,σ2) and Y∼N(ν,τ2)Y \sim N(\nu, \tau^2)Y∼N(ν,τ2) independently, then:

X+Y∼N(μ+ν,σ2+τ2)X + Y \sim N(\mu + \nu, \sigma^2 + \tau^2)X+Y∼N(μ+ν,σ2+τ2)
(1)

Sums of normals are normal. This is special — it doesn’t hold for most distributions.

Sampling Distributions — The Key Results¶

If we draw samples of size nnn from a population with mean μ\muμ and std σ\sigmaσ:

Mean:

Xˉ∼N ⁣(μ,σ2n)(exactly if population is normal)\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{(exactly if population is normal)}Xˉ∼N(μ,nσ2​)(exactly if population is normal)
(2)

Difference in means:

Xˉ1−Xˉ2∼N ⁣(μ1−μ2,σ12n1+σ22n2)\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2 \sim N\!\left(\mu_1 - \mu_2, \frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}\right)Xˉ1​−Xˉ2​∼N(μ1​−μ2​,n1​σ12​​+n2​σ22​​)
(3)

This is how the classical two-sample t-test is derived — it assumes both populations are normal and uses the above formula for the sampling distribution.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)¶

The most important theorem in statistics.

Statement: For any population with finite mean μ\muμ and variance σ2\sigma^2σ2, as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞:

Xˉ−μσ/n→dN(0,1)\frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)σ/n​Xˉ−μ​d​N(0,1)
(4)

The sample mean is approximately normally distributed, regardless of the shape of the population distribution, as long as nnn is large enough.

“Large enough” depends on skewness:

  • Symmetric population → n≈30n \approx 30n≈30 is usually fine

  • Moderately skewed → n≈100n \approx 100n≈100

  • Heavily skewed → n≈500+n \approx 500+n≈500+

Why does this matter? It is the reason normal-based formulas work in so many situations even when the data is not normal — we’re taking means of large samples, and means are approximately normal.

Testing the CLT¶

With NSFG birth weight (slightly left-skewed):

  • Population is NOT normal

  • Draw samples of size nnn, compute sample mean

  • As nnn increases, the distribution of sample means should approach normal

We can verify this by simulation — plot the sampling distribution of the mean for n=5,20,100,500n = 5, 20, 100, 500n=5,20,100,500. Watch it converge to normal.

When Simulation Beats Formulas¶

Use simulation (permutation test, bootstrap) when:

  1. The data is heavily skewed or has outliers (CLT hasn’t kicked in yet)

  2. The test statistic is not the mean (e.g., median, max, ratio)

  3. You have small samples (n<30n < 30n<30)

  4. The analytic formula requires assumptions you can’t verify

Use analytic methods when:

  1. nnn is large and data is not extremely skewed

  2. You need speed (simulation is 1000× slower)

  3. You want closed-form confidence intervals

  4. You need to communicate to an audience that expects p-values and t-statistics

Glossary¶

Central Limit Theorem (CLT) — the sample mean is approximately normal for large nnn, regardless of population shape

t-distribution — the sampling distribution of the mean when σ\sigmaσ is unknown; approaches normal as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞

t-test — hypothesis test based on the t-statistic; assumes (approximately) normal sampling distribution

Welch’s approximation — formula for degrees of freedom in two-sample t-test without assuming equal variances

chi-squared distribution — distribution of ∑(O−E)2/E\sum (O-E)^2/E∑(O−E)2/E under H0H_0H0​ for categorical data

analytic method — a formula-based result derived from probability theory (vs simulation-based)

normal approximation — using a normal distribution to approximate the sampling distribution when the CLT applies

convergence in distribution — a sequence of distributions approaching a limit distribution
