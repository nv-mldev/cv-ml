Part 2: The Binomial Distribution — Counting Successes

DhvaniAI

An Applied Scenario — One Second of Vibration Samples¶

Back to the motor from Part 1. The accelerometer streams 1,000 samples per second, and your threshold rule fires a 1 whenever a sample exceeds +2 g. On a healthy machine, calibration gave you p=0.005p = 0.005p=0.005 (1 in 200 samples is a transient).

You decide to summarise the stream one second at a time: count how many 1s show up in each 1,000-sample window. That count is the alarm metric the operator sees.

A few obvious questions:

  • What count should you expect on a healthy machine?

  • How much can the count drift from one second to the next without anything actually being wrong?

  • If you see a count of 20 in one window, is that worrying — or just normal variation?

You already have all the ingredients. Each window is n=1000n = 1000n=1000 independent Bernoulli trials with p=0.005p = 0.005p=0.005. The thing you’re counting — successes out of nnn trials — has a name.

Intuition¶

If a Bernoulli trial is one coin flip, the Binomial distribution answers: “If I run nnn independent trials, each with success probability ppp, how many successes will I get?”

  • Vibration window: in 1,000 samples, each with probability p=0.005p = 0.005p=0.005 of crossing the threshold, how many crossings kkk?

  • Manufacturing batch: if 200 units are produced and each has probability p=0.02p = 0.02p=0.02 of being defective, how many defects kkk?

  • Photon counting: if nnn photons strike a sensor and each has probability p=QEp = \text{QE}p=QE of being detected, how many electrons kkk?

In every case the structure is identical: nnn independent Bernoulli trials with the same ppp, count the successes.

The Math¶

P(k∣n,p)=(nk)pk(1−p)n−kP(k \mid n, p) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}P(k∣n,p)=(kn​)pk(1−p)n−k
(1)

where (nk)=n!k!(n−k)!\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}(kn​)=k!(n−k)!n!​ is the number of ways to choose which kkk out of nnn trials succeed.

Mean: E[k]=npE[k] = npE[k]=np Variance: Var(k)=np(1−p)\text{Var}(k) = np(1-p)Var(k)=np(1−p)

Breaking the formula down:

  • (nk)\binom{n}{k}(kn​) — how many ways can exactly kkk trials succeed out of nnn total?

  • pkp^kpk — probability that those kkk trials all succeed

  • (1−p)n−k(1-p)^{n-k}(1−p)n−k — probability that the remaining n−kn-kn−k trials all fail

  • Multiply: total probability of exactly kkk successes

Back to the Vibration Sensor¶

For the motor scenario:

n=1000,p=0.005⇒E[k]=5,Var(k)=4.975,σ≈2.23n = 1000, \quad p = 0.005 \quad\Rightarrow\quad E[k] = 5, \quad \text{Var}(k) = 4.975, \quad \sigma \approx 2.23n=1000,p=0.005⇒E[k]=5,Var(k)=4.975,σ≈2.23
(2)

So on a healthy machine you should see about 5 crossings per second, and a typical fluctuation of ±2\pm 2±2 or so. A count of 7 is unremarkable. A count of 20 is roughly 7σ7\sigma7σ above the mean — that’s not normal variation, that’s a state change worth investigating.

This is a real condition-monitoring rule built from one Bernoulli trial and one sum.

Visualizing the Binomial: Shape vs. nnn and ppp¶

The shape depends on both parameters.

  • Increasing nnn (fix ppp): the distribution widens and becomes more bell-shaped. This is the first hint of the Normal approximation and the CLT (Part 5).

  • Varying ppp (fix nnn): the peak shifts. The distribution is most symmetric when p=0.5p = 0.5p=0.5; it becomes skewed as ppp approaches 0 or 1.

For the motor case, p=0.005p = 0.005p=0.005 and n=1000n = 1000n=1000 — extremely small ppp, large nnn. The distribution is heavily skewed and concentrated near small counts. That regime — many trials, each rare — is exactly where the Binomial morphs into the Poisson distribution (Part 3).

Simulation: Repeated Windows¶

Simulating many 1-second windows from the same machine is the empirical version of the formula. Each simulated window is one independent set of nnn Bernoulli trials.

Why simulate when we have the formula? Simulation validates the theory and builds intuition. If simulation and formula disagree, one of them is wrong — a powerful debugging technique that survives all the way into deep learning, where closed-form answers stop existing and Monte Carlo is your only tool.

What to look for: the simulation histogram should hug the theoretical PMF. Small residual differences shrink as the number of simulated windows increases (also a CLT effect).
